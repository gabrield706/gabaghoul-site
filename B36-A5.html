Live Gig Blogpost

As far as an individual aesthetic, I’ve been thinking of using darkwave or Eastern-European post-punk-inspired aesthetics
as a sort of ‘jumping off point’ for my own. The whole ‘doomerwave’ internet subculture was kicked off by bands such as
Molchat Doma, who typically wear all-black outfits that include long coats, leather jackets, and black jeans or chinos.
All of their members also have facial hair, and while that’s not the reason for why I personally chose to grow out a beard,
I guess that’s an aesthetic plus for me now haha. Molchat Doma’s aesthetically definitely reminds me of my own to a certain degree;
I tend to wear a good amount of black clothing but in my case this comes more from a place of fashion minimalism and my own aesthetic laziness.
However, I am considering that I should maybe buy a leather jacket or long coat to spice things up a bit.

Given that dark, gothic techno sound that a lot of Molchat Doma’s songs tend to have, my guess is that their sound and aesthetic
might be largely influenced by some of the classic post-punk bands of the late 70s and early 80s that featured a similar sound
and aesthetic. In my list of top 10 artists I mention 2 of these classic goth/post-punk bands: Joy Division and The Cure.
Both of these bands were pretty much well-associated with their fashion sense in addition to their music: Joy Divison’s members
often wore button-up shirts and jeans (a more ‘working class’ aesthetic) while The Cure is not only notorious for Robert Smith’s
iconic hairstyle, but also for the black clothing their members would often wear. While you can see elements of both bands’ visual
aesthetics in the outfits of bands like Molchat Doma, I would however, follow those outfit choices to a certain degree. A lot of the
modern darkwave sounds are rooted in nostalgia for the more gothic sounds of the 80s and 90s. While I enjoy those types of sounds,
I would want my project to reflect our current time-or even something ahead of our time.

I know, I know, I previously mentioned wanting to move away form nostalgia. But at the same time I’ve been shaped by a lot of these 90s/2000s
future aesthetics that a lot of my aesthetic vision is shaped by media pieces like these. Growing up, I’ve been exposed to some particular
music videos inspired by industrial robotics and machinery. Now that AI is becoming more predominant in our everyday lives, I’ve been coming back
to these videos much more, and wouldn’t be surprised if this influences my creative direction with things like promotional tour art or even album covers.
I’ve also had these vague memories of futuristic fashion trends from the early 2000s, and have also seen a nostalgic resurgence for cyberwave-inspired fashion and design.
Outside of my own artistic outfit, I think I’m in a bit of a conundrum about how an aesthetic past will influence my own proposition for the future.
